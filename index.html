The Architectural Philosophy

A defining principle of this project was established during Warden Alwis' tenure: the Main Quadrangle is sacred. [cite_start]To honor our heritage, it was mandated that no new additions to the campus should be visible from that hallowed space[cite: 80, 81, 89].

[IMAGE: View of the Main Quadrangle showing how the new Senior Block remains hidden from view]
[cite_start]

Every new edifice is designed to be subservient to the main space[cite: 81]. [cite_start]This led to innovative solutions, such as cutting into the ground to the level of the dining hall for the Commerce Block, ensuring the building height respected the college’s historic aesthetic[cite: 92, 94].

The Visionaries

[cite_start]

The concept of the Senior Quadrangle was born from early dialogues that challenged the boundaries of traditional education while respecting the roots of the school[cite: 85].

"Do it as you think it should be done? But remember, the college has a tradition, a heritage."
— Warden de Soysa

Ashley de Vos

[cite_start]

Representing the STC Centenary Group, Ashley de Vos envisioned a "University Campus" methodology[cite: 75, 76, 100]. [cite_start]The goal was a single access spine where all senior disciplines could meet daily, fostering cross-disciplinary interaction[cite: 101, 102].

The Vision for 2030

[cite_start]

Moving away from the "tuition master" era, the quadrangle introduces 30'x30' classrooms designed for workstations, AI, and project-based learning, transforming the college into an "educational startup" environment[cite: 57, 68, 115].

The Benefactors

[cite_start]

This massive undertaking was made possible by the unwavering support of the Thomian community, notably the Gnanam family, whose contribution was pivotal in initiating the transformation[cite: 87, 96].

[IMAGE: The Commerce Block (Block I) - Funded by the Gnanam Foundation]
[cite_start]

The **Gnanam Foundation** undertook the total cost for Stage I in memory of the founder of the Gnanam empire, following a personal request from Warden de Soysa to his classmate[cite: 87, 88]. [cite_start]Today, Stages I, II, and III are fully completed and occupied, standing as a living legacy of this generosity[cite: 109, 110].

Symbols of Continuity

[cite_start]

At the heart of the new quadrangle sits a sapling from the original Banyan tree at Mutuwal, ensuring that even as we step into the future of IT and Robotics, we remain rooted in our origins[cite: 106, 115].

[cite_start]

Environmental preservation was key: during construction, the existing Beli tree was saved, and the old well was reinforced and secured as a historical feature[cite: 99, 127].
